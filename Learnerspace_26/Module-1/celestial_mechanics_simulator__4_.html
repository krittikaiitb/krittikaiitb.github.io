Section 01

The
Mathematical
Foundation

The equation of motion

Everything starts with F = ma

a  =  GMr²
dvdt  =  d²xdt²  =  GM₁M₂x²

If you can solve this differential then you can predict the position at every future moment.
It may look simple but it is deceptively difficult.

Two approaches

Solve it exactly
or approximate

Some systems have a clean, closed-form answer.
Most don't. So we approximate and iterate.

Analytical Solution
r(θ) = a(1−e²)/(1+e cosθ)
Will give you the exact solution
However, it's practical only for 2-body systems.
Numerical Solution
xₙ₊₁ = xₙ + vₙ·Δt
Approximate and iterative but it works for any system complexity.
Section 02

The
Two-Body
Problem

Two-body dynamics

They orbit each
other's barycenter

Two masses don't orbit each other, instead they both orbit their shared centre of mass. The heavier body barely wobbles while the lighter body swings wide.

Earth–Moon barycenter: 4,700 km from Earth's centre. (inside the Earth)
Sun–Jupiter barycenter: just above the Sun's surface.

What the analytical solution tells us

Kepler's Three Laws

  • Orbits are ellipses | the star sits at one focus, not the centre.
  • Equal areas, equal times | closer to the star = faster. Far away = slower.
  • T² ∝ a³ | period squared is proportional to orbit size cubed.

All three follow automatically from solving the equation of motion analytically.
Kepler found them empirically. Newton thenproved them from first principles.

Deriving the orbit equation — step 1

From force to geometry

We have the equation of motion. Now we want the shape of the orbit> The distance 'r' as a function of angle θ. The conservation of these two quantities makes this possible:

Conservation of Energy
E = 1 2 mv² − GMm r
Total mechanical energy stays constant throughout the orbit.
Conservation of Angular Momentum
L = mr² dθ dt = const
The planet sweeps equal areas in equal times. ← Kepler's 2nd Law, right here.

Because L is conserved, (dθ/dt = L/mr²) angular velocity is tied to r. This lets us change variables from time to angle.

Deriving the orbit equation — step 2

The substitution that cracks it open

Let u = 1/r. Rewrite the radial equation of motion in polar coordinates using u and θ. It now becomes the equation for a harmonic oscillator with a constant term.

Binet's Equation (the key result of the substitution)
d²u dθ² + u = GM (L/m)²
General solution (this is just y″ + y = const)
u(θ) = GM (L/m)² + C cosθ

C is an integration constant set by initial conditions. Visually it represents the elongation of the orbit.

Deriving the orbit equation — step 3

Substitute back: r = 1/u

Replace u with 1/r. Define eccentricity e = C·(L/m)²/GM and semi-latus rectum ℓ = (L/m)²/GM. Rearranging gives the orbit equation:

The Orbit Equation
r =
a(1 − e²) 1 + e cosθ
a — semi-major axis
Sets the size of the ellipse. Kepler's 3rd: T² ∝ a³ falls straight out.
e — eccentricity
e=0 → circle   e<1 → ellipse   e=1 → parabola   e>1 → hyperbola
θ — true anomaly
Angle from closest approach. At θ=0, r is minimum. Kepler's 1st: the shape is an ellipse.
What the derivation gave us for free

All three Kepler's Laws

1st Law — Ellipses
r = a(1−e²)/(1+e cosθ) is the polar equation of a conic section. For 0 < e < 1, it is an ellipse with the Sun at one focus. Emerges directly from the inverse-square law. If you change gravity to 1/r³ you'd get spirals instead of ellipses.
2nd Law — Equal Areas
L = mr²(dθ/dt) = const → dA/dt = L/2m = const. The rate of area swept is constant. This is just conservation of angular momentum, stated geometrically. No calculus needed to see it once you have L = const.
3rd Law — T² ∝ a³
Integrate dA/dt over one full period T. Area of ellipse = πab. So T = 2πab/(L/m). Plug in b = a√(1−e²) and ℓ = (L/m)²/GM: T² = (4π²/GM)·a³.Thus the period depends only on size and not shape.

Section 03

Chaos &
The Three-Body
Problem

Why study three bodies?

The Moon created a problem
Newton couldn't solve

The two-body problem is beautiful because it has an exact analytical solution. But the real Solar System is never made of only two objects. Even the Earth–Moon system is constantly perturbed by the Sun.

By the 1800s, astronomers wanted something extremely practical: predict the Moon's position with perfect accuracy. Accurate lunar predictions were essential for navigation, calendar keeping, and astronomy.

This led mathematicians to a deeper question:

If we can solve two gravitating bodies exactly, can we solve three?

The search for the Moon's motion would ultimately reveal the existence of chaos itself.

1888 — King Oscar II of Sweden

"Solve" the three-body problem
and win a prize

In 1888, King Oscar II of Sweden offered a prize for progress on the three-body problem. Henri Poincaré's entry transformed the field.

Instead of finding a general solution, he discovered something far more important: tiny differences in initial conditions can grow dramatically over time. His work revealed that deterministic equations can still produce behaviour that is fundamentally unpredictable.

The three-body problem became the birthplace of modern chaos theory.

Sensitive dependence on initial conditions

Change 0.1%.
Lose the planet.

Alter the initial position of one body by just 0.1%. A fraction of a millimetre in a planetary context and the long-term trajectory becomes completely different.

This is the butterfly effect, formalized for celestial mechanics.
This is not numerical error, the system itself is chaotic.

2009 Supercomputer Study
Move Mercury by 1 mm
Over 5 billion years, 1% of simulations ended with planets colliding or being ejected from the solar system.
The implication
The solar system's long-term stability is probabilistic, not guaranteed.
Writing down the three-body problem

Three bodies.
Nine equations.

Body 1
m₁ r̈₁ =
Gm₁m₂(r₂−r₁) |r₂−r₁|³
+
Gm₁m₃(r₃−r₁) |r₃−r₁|³
Bodies 2 & 3
m₂ r̈₂ = G
m₂m₁(r₁−r₂) |r₁−r₂|³
+ G
m₂m₃(r₃−r₂) |r₃−r₂|³
m₃ r̈₃ = G
m₃m₁(r₁−r₃) |r₁−r₃|³
+ G
m₃m₂(r₂−r₃) |r₂−r₃|³

9 coupled nonlinear ODEs. No closed-form solution exists.

Centre of mass — three-body

Known Periodic Solutions

Twenty islands of order
inside a chaotic ocean

For over 300 years, the three-body problem seemed completely hopeless. Then, beginning with the figure-eight solution discovered in 1993, mathematicians found a small collection of special trajectories where three bodies repeat their motion forever. Today, only about 20 families of stable periodic solutions are known.

Periodic Orbit — definition
Position(T) = Position(0) Velocity(T) = Velocity(0)
The entire system repeats forever.
Why it matters
These solutions prove that chaos is not the whole story. Even inside the three-body problem, tiny pockets of perfect order still exist.

Known stable periodic solutions of the three-body problem

The exceptions — Euler & Lagrange

Five parking spots
in the chaos

While no general three-body solution exists, there are 5 special points (L1–L5) where gravity and centrifugal force balance perfectly. A third small body can sit there stably.

L1, L2, L3 — on the line between the two bodies. Semi-stable (will drift if nudged).
L4, L5 — 60° ahead/behind in orbit. Fully stable. Jupiter has 10,000+ asteroids at L4/L5 (Trojans).

Section 04

Applied
Astrodynamics

The currency of spaceflight

Fuel is mass.
Mass is everything.

In space, we don't optimize for time. We optimize for Δv, the total change in velocity required. Every Δv costs propellant. More propellant = heavier rocket = needs more propellant to lift it.

Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation
Δv = ve · ln(m₀ / m_f)

Every extra m/s of Δv requires exponentially more fuel. Efficiency is survival.

Most fuel-efficient orbital manoeuvre

The Hohmann
Transfer

To move from a low orbit to a high orbit, you don't need to thrust continuously. You burn fuel only twice:

  • Burn 1 : accelerate into an ellipse that grazes the target orbit
  • Burn 2 : at the top, circularize into the new orbit

The transfer ellipse perfectly bridges the two circular orbits. Minimum Δv. Used for every satellite deployment and interplanetary mission.

Deep space propulsion — free of charge

Gravity Slingshot
(Gravity Assist)

Use a planet's gravity to accelerate by huge amounts. In the planet's frame, you enter and exit at the same speed (energy conserved). But in the Sun's frame, you steal a fraction of the planet's orbital momentum.

Voyager 1 used Jupiter + Saturn. Result: escape velocity from the solar system with engines made in the 1970s
No fuel burned.

Planet's frame
v_in = v_out
Energy is conserved. Speed unchanged on entry and exit.
Sun's frame
Δv_max = +2V_planet
Spacecraft gains up to twice the planet's orbital speed. Free velocity boost.
Numerical method 01 — Leonhard Euler, 1768

The Euler Method

dv/dt  =  a dv  =  a · dt Δv  =  a · Δt vnew = v₀ + a·Δt update velocity xnew = x₀ + v·Δt update position

Compute acceleration → nudge velocity → nudge position → repeat.
It's simple and elegant but there's a catch

The problem with Euler

Linear tangents
can't track curves

Euler uses a straight-line approximation for what is a curved path. Each step the planet is slightly wrong. These errors pile up and orbits spiral outward.

Error ∝ Δt  →  to halve error, halve Δt and double iterations.
Millions of steps just to simulate a few seconds.

Euler Error
ε ∝ Δt
Linear. Halve Δt → halve error. Needs tiny steps for higher accuracy, which makes it slow
What we need
ε ∝ (Δt)⁴
Quartic. Halve Δt → error drops 16×. This is accurate with large steps. Thus it takes less iterations
The gold standard | Runge-Kutta 4

RK4 : Four slopes,
one brilliant step

Instead of one tangent, RK4 samples the slope four times within a single Δt and takes a weighted average. Error: ε ∝ (Δt)⁴

If Euler needs Δt = 1s for stability, RK4 achieves the same accuracy at Δt = 10s.
That's 10× fewer calculations. Used in every serious physics simulator.

k₁ = f(t, y)
k₂ = f(t + ½Δt, y + ½k₁Δt)
k₃ = f(t + ½Δt, y + ½k₂Δt)
k₄ = f(t + Δt, y + k₃Δt)

ynext = y + ⅙(k₁+2k₂+2k₃+k₄)Δt

weighted average of four slope estimates

Section 05

Extreme
Gravitational
Phenomena

Differential gravity

Tidal Forces

Gravity weakens with distance (1/r²). The side of Earth facing the Moon is pulled harder than the far side. This stretches the oceans into an ellipse that causes two high tides per day.

Spring Tides
Sun + Moon aligned → pulls add together → extreme tides.
Neap Tides
Sun ⊥ Moon → pulls partially cancel → weaker tides.
Taken further
Tidal forces can exceed a body's own self-gravity → structural destruction.
When tidal force exceeds self-gravity

The Roche Limit

Every body has self-gravity holding it together. As it falls closer to a massive planet, differential gravity grows. At the Roche Limit, tidal force exceeds self-gravity and the body is torn apart.

Saturn's Rings
Theorized to be a moon that crossed the Roche limit and shattered into millions of fragments. All orbiting at the same distance.
Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9
Passed within Jupiter's Roche limit in 1992. Was torn into 21 fragments. Each crashed into Jupiter in 1994 which was observed from Earth.
A crack in Newton's theory

One planet refused
to obey Newton

For centuries, Newton's law of gravitation successfully explained the motion of planets, moons, comets, and tides. But there was one small problem.

Mercury's orbit slowly rotates through space. This is a phenomenon called perihelion precession. Most of this could be explained by gravitational tugs from other planets. Yet after accounting for every known effect, an unexplained discrepancy remained:

43 arcseconds
per century — unexplained

The error was tiny. But in celestial mechanics, tiny errors matter. Either the observations were wrong or Newton's theory was incomplete.

The solution arrived in 1915, when Einstein replaced gravity as a force with gravity as the curvature of spacetime.

A new picture of gravity

A new picture
of gravity

Newton imagined gravity as a force acting across space.
Einstein proposed something radically different.

Newton
Gravity is a force that pulls masses toward each other across empty space.
Einstein
Mass and energy curve spacetime itself. Planets follow the straightest possible paths through curved geometry.

To understand black holes, gravitational waves, and the orbit of Mercury,
we must leave Newton's universe behind.

General Relativity — Einstein, 1915

Mass warps
spacetime itself

Newton's gravity is a force. Einstein's gravity is geometry. Mass curves the fabric of spacetime and objects follow the straightest possible path through curved space : geodesics.

A black hole concentrates so much mass that spacetime curves infinitely. Not even light can follow a path that leads out. The escape velocity exceeds c.

Gravitational lensing

Light bends.
Galaxies form rings.

A black hole between Earth and a distant galaxy bends the galaxy's light around it from all directions. The result: a perfect circular mirage called an Einstein Ring.

Predicted by Einstein in 1915. First observed in 1988.
Now used to detect dark matter and measure cosmic distances.
